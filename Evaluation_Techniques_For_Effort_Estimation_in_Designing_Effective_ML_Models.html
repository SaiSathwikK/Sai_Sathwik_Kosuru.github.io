Synopsis

Machine Learning Research often involves the use of diverse libraries, modules, and pseudocodes for data processing, cleaning, filtering, pattern recognition, and computer intelligence. Quantization of effort required for these cumulative processes is rarely discussed in existing works. The time to reach the desired level of the modelâ€™s functionality is essential to gauge the environment for model training and pre-deployment testing. In this study, we empirically defined the manual-cum-computational effort required for model development in terms of two-time factors: time-to-live (time until the first code modification) and time-for-modification (time between two codebase changes). These factors are taken together in a mathematical model to compute the Effort Factor, a quantitative measure of the effort needed to design ML algorithms.

This study is novel in its approach to calculating the effort required to create ML models, with a particular focus on manual effort direction. The results and findings can be used to determine the total time needed to synthesize ML models and frameworks in terms of code change cycles and implementation strategies. Notably, the study marks a 25% performance increase in the current method above the standard pipeline.

The empirical approach adopted in this research highlights the importance of understanding and quantifying the effort involved in developing machine learning models. By focusing on both manual and computational aspects, the study provides a comprehensive framework for assessing the efficiency of ML model development. This approach not only aids in better resource allocation and project planning but also contributes to the optimization of development workflows. The insights gained from this study could be instrumental in enhancing the performance and scalability of machine learning applications, ultimately leading to more robust and efficient computational systems.
